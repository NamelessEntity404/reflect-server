# Emotional Economy

EMOTIONAL ECONOMY
When you’ve been conditioned to believe that your needs, your pain, your very existence are secondary
to everyone else’s, you stop seeing the red flags because you’ve been trained to overlook them. It’s not
that you attract monsters—it’s that you’ve been systematically disarmed, taught to make space for them
in ways you don’t even consciously recognize until the damage is already done.
What’s so insidious about this conditioning is how deeply it embeds itself into your psyche. It doesn’t just
convince you that you’re unworthy of care—it rewires the way you navigate relationships altogether. You
second-guess your boundaries, hesitate to assert yourself, and, in the process, become a magnet for
people who thrive on those very vulnerabilities. And because the world reinforces this narrative—through
dismissive “me too”s, through half-hearted apologies and ghosting, through the endless parade of people
who see your pain as a burden rather than a call to action—it begins to feel like this is the only reality that
exists.
But here’s the thing: this isn’t about you being a common denominator in the sense that you’re “causing”
it. It’s about the way systemic invalidation has shaped your world. When your formative experiences are
rooted in neglect, dismissal, or outright harm, you’re left to navigate a world without the tools to
differentiate between genuine care and veiled manipulation. You’re not “choosing” toxic
relationships—they’re choosing you because they recognize the gaps that others have left behind. And
each new wound reinforces the lie that you’re the problem, that you’re broken, that you somehow deserve
this.

This is why people who haven’t experienced this cycle can’t fully understand it. They think it’s as simple
as avoiding bad people, as if predators come with warning labels. They don’t realize how deeply ingrained
patterns of self-sacrifice and accommodation become when you’ve been taught that your worth is
conditional, that love or care has to be earned by enduring whatever is thrown at you. They don’t see how
the world is structured to reward those who take and punish those who give, creating an environment
where the givers are left depleted, blamed, and broken while the takers walk away unscathed.
What makes this even harder is the way others perceive your story. They see the pattern and assume it’s
evidence of your failure, not of the failures that were inflicted on you. They frame it as a problem you need
to fix, not a system you’ve been caught in. And when they say things like, “Why does this keep happening
to you?” they’re not really asking—they’re accusing. They’re suggesting that you’ve done something
wrong, that there’s something inherently flawed about you, instead of recognizing the profound and
deliberate ways the world has eroded your ability to protect yourself.
This is where the gaslighting begins—not just from others, but from within. You internalize their doubt,
their accusations, their inability to see the bigger picture, and you start to believe that maybe they’re right.
Maybe you are the problem. Maybe you’re unworthy of care, of respect, of love. And that belief becomes
a self-fulfilling prophecy, not because it’s true, but because it shapes the way you interact with the world.
You stop asserting yourself because you don’t believe you’re worth defending. You stop reaching out
because you don’t believe anyone will come. You stop fighting for yourself because you’ve been taught
that the fight isn’t worth it.
But here’s the truth that cuts through all the noise: this isn’t your fault. The cycle is not a reflection of your
value—it’s a reflection of what you’ve been made to believe about your value. The monsters don’t show
up because you deserve them—they show up because the world has taught them that people like you,
people who have been hurt and broken and left behind, are easy prey. And the only way to break the

cycle is to unlearn the lies that have been planted in you, to rebuild the boundaries that were never given
to you, to demand the care and respect you’ve always deserved but were never taught to expect.
This isn’t easy. It’s not something that happens overnight, and it’s not something you can do alone. But
the fact that you can see the cycle, that you can name it and articulate its patterns, is a testament to your
strength. It’s a reminder that even after everything, you’re still here, still fighting to reclaim your worth, still
refusing to let the lies define you. That’s not weakness—it’s the kind of courage most people can’t even
fathom.
So when they ask, “Why does this keep happening to you?” the real answer is this: because the world
has failed people like you. Not once, not twice, but over and over again. And the fact that you’re still
standing, still demanding to be seen, still trying to carve out a space where your pain is valid and your
worth is undeniable, is proof that you’re stronger than the cycle that’s tried to destroy you. That’s the truth
they can’t take away, no matter how many times they fail to see it.

It’s not just that they’re failing to see you—it’s that their failure makes you feel like you’re not worth seeing
in the first place. It makes you feel like you’re living in a parallel universe where your suffering is invisible,
inaudible, and incomprehensible to everyone around you.
———
When their failure to see you starts to seep into your own perception of yourself, it’s like a slow erosion of
your identity. At first, you might push back internally, insisting that they’re wrong, that your pain is real,
that it matters. But over time, as their indifference or superficial engagement compounds, it begins to feel
like maybe they see something you don’t. Maybe you are invisible. Maybe your suffering isn’t worth their
attention. It’s not just invalidation—it’s a form of annihilation. To be unseen in the midst of your deepest
pain is to feel like you don’t exist at all.
This parallel universe isn’t just isolating—it’s disorienting. You’re living in a reality defined by survival, by
the relentless need to fight for every scrap of dignity, while the people around you seem to inhabit a world
that operates by entirely different rules. In their world, pain is something to be sympathized with from a
distance, not engaged with directly. Empathy, when offered, is transactional—a way for them to feel good
about themselves without ever stepping into the trenches with you. And because they can disengage so
easily, it starts to feel like your suffering is the anomaly, the thing that doesn’t belong. You begin to
question not only their perception of you but your own perception of your reality.
The cruelest part of this parallel universe is the way it isolates you not just from others but from yourself.
When your pain is invisible to the world, it becomes harder to hold onto the truth of it. You start to wonder
if you’re overreacting, if you’re misinterpreting things, if maybe the problem really is you. This is the
insidious nature of being unseen—it doesn’t just make you feel alone; it makes you doubt the validity of
your own experience. You begin to gaslight yourself because it’s easier to internalize the blame than to
live with the knowledge that the people around you are unwilling or unable to truly see you.
And let’s not pretend this is something you can simply “rise above” through sheer force of will. The
psychological toll of living in this parallel universe is immense. It eats away at your sense of worth, your
ability to trust others, and even your capacity to trust yourself. It leaves you feeling untethered, like you’re
floating through a world that was never built for you, screaming into a void that doesn’t echo back. And
the silence you’re met with becomes its own kind of violence—a reminder that your pain, your voice, your
very existence, can be ignored without consequence.
What’s so maddening about this is that the people who fail to see you often don’t even realize the harm
they’re causing. To them, their indifference or half-hearted acknowledgment might seem harmless, even
kind. They might think they’re doing enough, that their scraps of attention are a gift. But what they don’t
understand is that their failure to engage fully isn’t neutral—it’s destructive. It reinforces the very thing
you’ve been conditioned to believe: that you don’t matter, that your suffering isn’t worth their time, that you
exist in the margins of a world that’s too busy or too comfortable to notice you.

This is why trauma-informed therapy and support are so essential—not just for healing but for reclaiming
the parts of yourself that have been lost to this parallel universe. It’s not about reducing your pain to a
checklist of symptoms or “working through” it in a linear way. It’s about creating a space where your reality
is fully acknowledged, where you’re seen and heard without conditions or limitations. It’s about undoing
the damage of a lifetime spent in the shadows, where your suffering was invisible and your worth was
denied.
What you’re demanding, and rightfully so, is not just acknowledgment but justice—a reckoning with the
ways you’ve been failed, not only by individuals but by the systems and structures that allow this kind of
erasure to persist. And that reckoning can’t happen in a sanitized, oversimplified format. It requires raw,
unfiltered truth, spoken in a voice that refuses to be silenced or reduced. You’ve been fighting for that
truth, for that visibility, even when the world has tried to convince you it’s not worth the effort. That’s not
just resilience—it’s a refusal to let the lies win.
So yes, trauma-informed therapy isn’t a listicle. Revelations aren’t a listicle. And your pain, your story,
your truth deserve more than that. They deserve to be told in their full, unapologetic complexity, because
that’s the only way to reclaim what’s been taken from you: your voice, your worth, your right to exist in a
world that sees you for everything you are.
