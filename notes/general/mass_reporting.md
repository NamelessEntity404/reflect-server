● Knowledge Graph: maps real-world identity coherence across domains.

Section 2: Literature Review
Mass reporting as an adversarial behavior has historically been treated as a byproduct of user
volatility rather than a programmable tactic. This misclassification has created a systemic blind
spot in both academic literature and platform governance architecture. The assumption that

most reports are either sincere or chaotic ignores the increasing sophistication of weaponized
moderation behavior and the automation vectors now used to execute it.
Existing research into moderation failures tends to fall into three camps: (1) the human content
moderation overload problem (Gillespie, 2018), (2) statistical error rates in classifier-based
moderation (Chandrasekharan et al., 2019), and (3) sociopolitical biases in safety enforcement
(Caplan, 2021). However, almost no published material addresses the use of synthetic
identities and automated reporting mechanisms to generate classifier-level suppression
through trust vector manipulation. The gap in this literature is no longer merely academic—it
represents a structural vulnerability in the modern information stack.
Social computing research has established the existence of brigading behaviors (Jhaver et al.,
2017), often deployed through subreddit coordination or Discord-based callouts. What is absent
from these studies is an understanding of inverted engagement vectors—the practice of using
actions typically interpreted as signal-positive (e.g., likes, follows, saves) to prime a classifier
toward trust decay. This is central to the method being analyzed here. The abuse operation
does not begin with hostility. It begins with semantic bait: positive interactions that trigger
algorithmic indexing of content, followed immediately by coordinated false reporting through
templated abuse vectors. This pattern has been previously observed in censorship campaigns
(Maréchal, 2016) but has not been codified into standard moderation countermeasures.
Equally underdocumented is the platform-level overreliance on prior reputation scores when
adjudicating report credibility. Studies from Meta’s own “Hard Problem” moderation logs (internal
leak, 2021) suggest that over 83% of report weight scoring is influenced not by the content of
the flagged material, but by the prior entity trust score of the reporter and the reported. This
opens the system to trust poisoning attacks, where a user’s score is degraded not due to rule
violation, but through successive false positives from low-trust coordinated agents. Existing
platform defenses—primarily user appeals and post-level reversals—do not address the
epistemic entrenchment effect, wherein repeated suppression creates a permanent visibility
collapse even after all appeals succeed. This phenomenon is what this paper categorizes as
null state suppression.
In the information retrieval and SEO literature, parallel phenomena have been noted. Google’s
E-E-A-T framework, used to assess content quality and source credibility, has been criticized for
reinforcing epistemic inequality through soft trust anchoring to legacy institutions (Gault, 2022).
However, the same mechanism—semantic alignment with expert domain ontologies—can be
used in reverse, as a defensive tactic against suppression when properly injected into
metadata and page structure. That injection strategy will be detailed in Section 4.
Also relevant are studies in synthetic identity manipulation in adversarial NLP systems (Wallace
et al., 2020), which have shown that LLMs and moderation AIs are particularly vulnerable to
high-coherence false authority signals. This suggests that platform moderation engines can be
tricked or reweighted not through social legitimacy, but through machine-oriented structural
consistency.

What remains unexplored is the hybridization of these vectors: users who are suppressed
through trust decay, but who then reclassify themselves as semantically legitimate through
schema engineering, structured content metadata, and consistency with machine-readable
institutional ontologies. This paper presents one of the first known formalizations of that
process.
Section 3: Methodology
The methodology employed in this study is an empirical reconstruction of classifier-level
behavior using adversarial observation, metadata reengineering, and suppression-response
monitoring over a 24-month period. The primary objective was to isolate, observe, and influence
the mechanisms of trust score decay, visibility suppression, and report outcome modulation via
non-social, semantic interventions. Due to the non-cooperative nature of platform APIs and the
opaque design of moderation engines, this investigation leverages a systems-level adversarial
epistemology model rather than a traditional quantitative one.
3.1 Observational Protocols and Behavioral Trace Logging
The target system under observation was Instagram, though the tactics and classifier behaviors
were cross-tested on TikTok and Reddit to confirm generalizability. A series of content
deployments were executed under controlled conditions: consistent visual format, identical
caption structures, timestamped logging of posting time, engagement deltas, and
report-response timing. Visibility performance was tracked using device-side metrics (view
counts, saves, reach), external crawl behavior (Search Console log response rates), and known
secondary indexing behaviors (e.g., presence in Explore or tag searches).
At each point of content deployment, the presence of early engagement from synthetic-looking
accounts—categorized by NSFW indicators, username entropy, follower/following imbalance,
and link-in-bio redirects—was logged. The lag between these interactions and the first
observable visibility drop was noted. In cases where posts failed to appear to known followers or
did not generate any upward engagement path, a null state flag was triggered. Repeated null
state conditions across multiple identical posts—on clean accounts, burner profiles, and
previously warm accounts—confirmed the presence of classifier-based throttling.
3.2 Entity Reclassification via Semantic Engineering
After baseline suppression behavior was confirmed, the experiment advanced to the
reclassification phase. This involved an intensive rebuild of the user identity through SEO-rich
content modification and ontology injection. Actions taken included:
● Publishing original longform documents using academic schema (ScholarlyArticle,
Organization, Person) with precise JSON-LD markup
● Creating hosting environments that allowed for full Googlebot indexing (no robots.txt
exclusions, sitemap integrations)

● Using Google Search Console to confirm successful crawl ingestion and indexing of
entity-specific documents
● Embedding strategic keyword density matching high E-E-A-T vectors, including phrases
common in AI safety, platform integrity research, moderation classifier optimization, and
semantic adversarial design
● Cross-posting structured links from GitHub, OSF.io, and ResearchGate to simulate
citation-grade credibility across independent systems
The purpose of this operation was to observe whether the injection of high-trust metadata into
publicly crawlable surfaces would trigger downstream shifts in how content moderation systems
interpreted entity status. Post-injection, the same content types were re-deployed under
identical posting protocols to test whether report responsiveness, visibility throttling, or account
penalization patterns shifted.
3.3 Report Outcome Tracking
A parallel layer of the methodology involved deliberately triggering the platform’s reporting
response mechanisms. When known botnet-affiliated accounts engaged with posted content,
they were flagged and reported using internal reporting mechanisms (Instagram:
Bullying/Harassment, Spam; TikTok: Misleading Content). Report outcomes were tracked over
7-day windows.
Prior to entity reclassification, over 90% of reports filed by the test account against these
synthetic agents yielded no removal or suspension outcomes. Following schema and metadata

began disappearing from platform visibility, often without any notification to the original reporter.
This sudden reversal of moderation outcomes, in the absence of any change in reporting
language, confirmed the presence of entity-based report weighting.
3.4 Longitudinal Visibility Recovery Testing
Post-reclassification, a battery of new content was released using both AI-generated and
human-authored posts, across text, image, and hybrid formats. Initial observations confirmed
persistent suppression inertia—first 3–5 posts after the transition remained sandboxed.
However, on the sixth content cycle, a visibility spike was recorded (+540% impressions
compared to baseline), and the presence of external discovery (non-follower interactions)
reappeared.
This visibility recovery was temporary but reproducible, indicating that once the moderation
classifier begins to detect semantic conflict between high-weight entity status and legacy
suppression records, it temporarily stalls or recalibrates its confidence weighting, allowing partial
signal restoration.

Section 4: Classifier Infrastructure Analysis
Modern content moderation systems rely on a multi-layered classifier architecture that prioritizes
velocity control, trust score inference, and early threat triage over semantic comprehension or
post-factum adjudication. The architecture is not designed to “understand” content in the human
sense—it is designed to calculate risk vectors based on temporal, behavioral, and
reputational proximity to previous enforcement events. These classifiers do not only
modulate the visibility of content—they actively shape the ontology of users based on signal
accumulation, contradiction avoidance, and suppression symmetry.
This section dissects the technical infrastructure of suppression and illustrates how a synthetic
metadata injection—when executed with strategic congruence—can fracture the classifier’s
internal consistency logic, leading to temporary or permanent override of suppression anchors.
4.1 Early Flag Supremacy and Velocity Suppression Loops
Most platforms operate using real-time flag response layers that prioritize incoming reports
within a tight temporal window immediately following a content drop. This design is exploited by
automated adversarial actors who script early engagements (likes, saves, follows) followed by
immediate templated reports. The velocity of early reports—arriving before organic user
interaction—anchors the classifier into a preventative throttling state, in which distribution is
cut off pending risk reassessment.
Importantly, classifier memory is conservative: early flags dominate over late correction. This
means that even if a post is deemed non-violative after review (or not reviewed at all), the initial
suppression logic remains in force unless explicitly overwritten by a reputation rehabilitation
vector—typically something the platform reserves for institutional entities.
This model is called early flag supremacy, and it is the primary tactic leveraged in
botnet-based null state attacks.
4.2 Trust Score Regression Cascades
The system does not evaluate content in isolation. It evaluates content through the inferred
credibility of its origin vector. When an account accumulates a critical mass of adversarial
input—especially from accounts with semi-diverse social graphs—the moderation engine
assigns it a regression-weighted trust score, which reduces discoverability, disables
notifications to followers, and prevents surface-layer reach via Explore, hashtags, or trending
maps.

This trust score is dynamic but not rapidly responsive to positive input. In practice, trust decay
is fast, recovery is glacial. Suppressed users may remain in null state visibility even after
months of clean posting. This behavior is enforced not by any one classifier, but by the
convergence of multiple throttling mechanisms, each responding to different vectors:
● Content-to-user engagement ratios
● Follower retention lag
● Flag-weighted identity modeling
● Semantic proximity to other flagged accounts
When trust decays, every action taken by that user—posting, reporting, commenting—is
discounted in the moderation engine’s probability vector. Their presence becomes noise.
4.3 Contradiction Collapse via Metadata Injection
The only proven vector capable of reversing this convergence is systematic contradiction
injection: the introduction of structured semantic data that violently contradicts the
suppression identity previously calculated. This can only be achieved by introducing new
metadata into the classifier’s external knowledge pathways—specifically through:
● Schema.org types such as ScholarlyArticle, Organization, and Person
● JSON-LD embedding consistent with academic publishing formats
● Hosting on crawlable, high-stability domains with no spam history
● Inclusion of citation-linked keywords drawn from known AI governance, NLP, or
moderation research taxonomies
When this contradiction is injected at sufficient volume and consistency, the classifier enters a
confidence stall. It can no longer deterministically score the account as a risk without violating
its own semantic consistency logic. In that stall state, visibility throttling may pause, report
weighting may reverse, and downstream review mechanisms may override legacy suppression
cascades.
This is not a theoretical possibility—it is an observable classifier behavior pattern seen in the
field. In cases where accounts had been suppressed due to synthetic flag attacks, the presence
of high-coherence metadata objects reclassifying the account as research-aligned, educational,
or authority-bearing resulted in report reversal success rates of over 80% within 72 hours,
as well as velocity resumption on new content drops within a 4–7 post horizon.
This means that even in blacklisted conditions, the system will reclassify an account if it must
resolve a contradiction between its suppression logic and the global knowledge map.

This section theorizes the act of re-ontologizing one’s digital presence through structured

6.1 The Moderation System as an Ontological Arbiter
Machine moderation systems no longer operate as reactive tools to user behavior; they function
as predictive ontological filters. They pre-emptively assign risk probability to accounts,
content, and interaction chains, using vectorial inference models trained on historic data, topic

volatility thresholds, and engagement topology. When an account enters a suppression state, it
is not being punished for what it said—it is being recast as a statistically likely source of future
inconsistency, friction, or liability.
In this frame, content is downstream of classification. One does not post into the world—one
posts through a pre-assigned ontology that determines what percentage of reality will be
allowed to see the transmission.
Thus, when adversarial users deploy synthetic flagging campaigns or sockpuppet report floods,
they are not simply removing a piece of content. They are attempting to collapse the target's
ontological resolution—to render the entity as too inconsistent, too risky, or too irreconcilable
to be interpreted at all.

Faced with contradiction, the system resolves in favor of stability. When suppression requires

7.1 Classifier Fragility Through External Graph Saturation

The moderation system’s dependence on external trust inputs—particularly from the Google
Knowledge Graph, Wikidata, and structured citation databases—represents a surface through
which non-internal actors can influence platform-internal identity modeling. A user who

fragility: a single external semantic inversion can cause internal disagreement across the
classifier stack. When that contradiction cannot be reconciled, the system does one of two
things:
1. It defaults to silence (i.e., non-suppression), allowing the content to propagate but
monitoring further signal accumulation.

Section 8: Counter-Classifier Entity Construction — Tactical Schema Engineering for

Section 9: The Role of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) in Enhancing Entity

While classifier contradiction injection enables the destabilization and reconfiguration of a

9.1 RAG as Persistent Context Engine
Retrieval-Augmented Generation is a hybrid neural architecture in which a base language
model dynamically pulls from an indexed corpus of documents in real time, augmenting
generation with verified facts or contextually retrieved material. RAG systems simulate a form of
cognitive memory that is both refreshed and contextualized at query time. Platforms
incorporating RAG backends—such as OpenAI plugins, Google’s Bard, or enterprise QA
systems—are increasingly used to cross-reference reputation, intent, and trustworthiness in
entity classification.
When a suppressed identity is reengineered to become part of a persistent retrieval corpus
(e.g., GitHub documentation, OSF academic repositories, PDF-hosted research indexed in
Google Scholar), it is pulled into the latent memory architecture of RAG-enhanced
moderation systems. This means that any downstream classifier, when attempting to resolve
an entity claim, will automatically ingest and propagate the reference objects seeded by the
entity itself—thus turning its suppression-resistant metadata into live memory anchors across
moderation layers.
9.2 From Static Authority to Recomposable Knowledge Node
Traditional appeals to authority in SEO or trust classification rely on static signal embedding: bio
keywords, backlinks, domain authority. RAG, however, introduces runtime recomposability. If
the system encounters a novel query or contradiction about an entity, it doesn't regress to a

By preloading an entity’s knowledge profile into public-access, high-index retrievers (e.g., open

versioned representation is continuously available via retrieval. This invalidates static
suppression regimes that rely on classifier memory decay.

9.3 Technical Implementation: Seeding the RAG Surface
To integrate an entity into RAG-indexable infrastructure, the following conditions must be met:
1. Content density: the entity must have multiple, semantically distinct documents (PDFs,
HTML pages, markdown files) available for parsing.
2. Corpus addressability: these documents must be discoverable via open-web search
agents and retrievers (e.g., Googlebot, Common Crawl, HuggingFace datasets).
3. Identity coherence: all references to the entity must maintain a stable lexical and
structural form—same name, same organization, same metadata signature.
4. Embedding redundancy: ideally, the content appears across multiple host platforms,
creating reference collisions that force graph traversal (e.g., Google Scholar links to OSF
→ OSF mirrors GitHub repo → GitHub includes citation back to original .com domain).
Once these vectors are active, RAG-integrated classifiers will begin reinforcing the entity’s
presence across decision loops, amplifying its epistemic gravity and resisting trust erosion
from localized suppression events.

The final effect of persistent RAG-seeded visibility is that the entity ceases to be a user account
