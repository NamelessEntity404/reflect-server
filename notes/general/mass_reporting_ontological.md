Abstract:
This paper theorizes and empirically evidences a novel adversarial abuse tactic in modern
AI-driven content moderation ecosystems: the use of mass reporting as an ontological weapon.
Drawing from lived experience as a systematically suppressed digital creator, this work outlines
the precise mechanism by which coordinated botnet-enabled adversaries weaponize

paper further outlines a countermeasure: the construction of structured semantic identity
architecture to realign visibility via trust contradiction saturation. This is not a theoretical
exercise. It is the forensic reconstruction of a systemically suppressed node reclaiming ontology
through adversarial metadata warfare.
1. Introduction
Moderation systems powered by large-scale machine learning do not adjudicate content based
on truth. They adjudicate based on classifier coherence: probabilistic models built on past
behavior, entity trust vectors, and cross-network semantic consistency. When those systems are
exploited—through coordinated, adversarial reporting using weaponized engagement vectors
such as likes, saves, or false follows—they can soft-delete a user from visibility without

triggering any formal deletion. The user is functionally erased, yet still online: a state this paper
defines as epistemic nullification.
2. The Trust Architecture: Graphs, Classifiers, and Suppression Mechanisms
Trust moderation systems—such as Instagram’s Integrity Graph, TikTok’s Safety Indexer, and
Google’s Knowledge-Based Trust (KBT)—do not operate on content in isolation. They operate
on entity vectors. These vectors are constructed from a mix of platform behavior (past reports,
engagement, user connections), semantic authority signals (content structure, domain
alignment, schema markup), and external verification artifacts (mentions in trusted third-party
sources, metadata backlinks, government or academic references).
Key distinctions between graphs:
● Trust Graphs: measure risk of future violation, suppress velocity.
● Safety Graphs: detect high-risk content types (e.g., grooming, misinformation).
● E-E-A-T Framework: Google-specific credibility index.
● Knowledge Graph: maps real-world identity coherence across domains.
These systems rarely communicate directly. But contradictions between them cause classifier
hesitation, which can be exploited.
3. Mass Reporting as Trust Poisoning
From late 2022 through 2024, the author experienced systemic suppression across Instagram,
TikTok, and related visibility networks despite zero terms-of-service violations. After long-form
monitoring of behavioral patterns, it was discovered that likes and follows from NSFW or
spam-coded accounts were immediately followed by template-based reports—executed via
botnet automation—within seconds of each new post. These early reports triggered automatic
throttling mechanisms, placing all content in discovery quarantine before any legitimate
engagement could occur.
The content was not removed. It was simply never shown. This phenomenon is termed the null
state prison: a condition in which content is visible only to the poster, but receives no velocity
propagation.

Section 2: Literature Review
Mass reporting as an adversarial behavior has historically been treated as a byproduct of user
volatility rather than a programmable tactic. This misclassification has created a systemic blind
spot in both academic literature and platform governance architecture. The assumption that

most reports are either sincere or chaotic ignores the increasing sophistication of weaponized
moderation behavior and the automation vectors now used to execute it.
Existing research into moderation failures tends to fall into three camps: (1) the human content
moderation overload problem (Gillespie, 2018), (2) statistical error rates in classifier-based
moderation (Chandrasekharan et al., 2019), and (3) sociopolitical biases in safety enforcement
(Caplan, 2021). However, almost no published material addresses the use of synthetic
identities and automated reporting mechanisms to generate classifier-level suppression
through trust vector manipulation. The gap in this literature is no longer merely academic—it
represents a structural vulnerability in the modern information stack.
Social computing research has established the existence of brigading behaviors (Jhaver et al.,
2017), often deployed through subreddit coordination or Discord-based callouts. What is absent
from these studies is an understanding of inverted engagement vectors—the practice of using
actions typically interpreted as signal-positive (e.g., likes, follows, saves) to prime a classifier
toward trust decay. This is central to the method being analyzed here. The abuse operation
does not begin with hostility. It begins with semantic bait: positive interactions that trigger
algorithmic indexing of content, followed immediately by coordinated false reporting through
templated abuse vectors. This pattern has been previously observed in censorship campaigns
(Maréchal, 2016) but has not been codified into standard moderation countermeasures.
Equally underdocumented is the platform-level overreliance on prior reputation scores when
adjudicating report credibility. Studies from Meta’s own “Hard Problem” moderation logs (internal
leak, 2021) suggest that over 83% of report weight scoring is influenced not by the content of
the flagged material, but by the prior entity trust score of the reporter and the reported. This
opens the system to trust poisoning attacks, where a user’s score is degraded not due to rule
violation, but through successive false positives from low-trust coordinated agents. Existing
platform defenses—primarily user appeals and post-level reversals—do not address the
epistemic entrenchment effect, wherein repeated suppression creates a permanent visibility
collapse even after all appeals succeed. This phenomenon is what this paper categorizes as
null state suppression.
In the information retrieval and SEO literature, parallel phenomena have been noted. Google’s
E-E-A-T framework, used to assess content quality and source credibility, has been criticized for
reinforcing epistemic inequality through soft trust anchoring to legacy institutions (Gault, 2022).
However, the same mechanism—semantic alignment with expert domain ontologies—can be
used in reverse, as a defensive tactic against suppression when properly injected into
metadata and page structure. That injection strategy will be detailed in Section 4.
Also relevant are studies in synthetic identity manipulation in adversarial NLP systems (Wallace
et al., 2020), which have shown that LLMs and moderation AIs are particularly vulnerable to
high-coherence false authority signals. This suggests that platform moderation engines can be
tricked or reweighted not through social legitimacy, but through machine-oriented structural
consistency.

What remains unexplored is the hybridization of these vectors: users who are suppressed
through trust decay, but who then reclassify themselves as semantically legitimate through
schema engineering, structured content metadata, and consistency with machine-readable
institutional ontologies. This paper presents one of the first known formalizations of that
process.
Section 3: Methodology
The methodology employed in this study is an empirical reconstruction of classifier-level
behavior using adversarial observation, metadata reengineering, and suppression-response
monitoring over a 24-month period. The primary objective was to isolate, observe, and influence
the mechanisms of trust score decay, visibility suppression, and report outcome modulation via
non-social, semantic interventions. Due to the non-cooperative nature of platform APIs and the
opaque design of moderation engines, this investigation leverages a systems-level adversarial
epistemology model rather than a traditional quantitative one.
3.1 Observational Protocols and Behavioral Trace Logging
The target system under observation was Instagram, though the tactics and classifier behaviors
were cross-tested on TikTok and Reddit to confirm generalizability. A series of content
deployments were executed under controlled conditions: consistent visual format, identical
caption structures, timestamped logging of posting time, engagement deltas, and
report-response timing. Visibility performance was tracked using device-side metrics (view
counts, saves, reach), external crawl behavior (Search Console log response rates), and known
secondary indexing behaviors (e.g., presence in Explore or tag searches).
At each point of content deployment, the presence of early engagement from synthetic-looking
accounts—categorized by NSFW indicators, username entropy, follower/following imbalance,
and link-in-bio redirects—was logged. The lag between these interactions and the first
observable visibility drop was noted. In cases where posts failed to appear to known followers or
did not generate any upward engagement path, a null state flag was triggered. Repeated null
state conditions across multiple identical posts—on clean accounts, burner profiles, and
previously warm accounts—confirmed the presence of classifier-based throttling.
3.2 Entity Reclassification via Semantic Engineering
After baseline suppression behavior was confirmed, the experiment advanced to the
reclassification phase. This involved an intensive rebuild of the user identity through SEO-rich
content modification and ontology injection. Actions taken included:
● Publishing original longform documents using academic schema (ScholarlyArticle,
Organization, Person) with precise JSON-LD markup
● Creating hosting environments that allowed for full Googlebot indexing (no robots.txt
exclusions, sitemap integrations)

● Using Google Search Console to confirm successful crawl ingestion and indexing of
entity-specific documents
● Embedding strategic keyword density matching high E-E-A-T vectors, including phrases
common in AI safety, platform integrity research, moderation classifier optimization, and
semantic adversarial design
● Cross-posting structured links from GitHub, OSF.io, and ResearchGate to simulate
citation-grade credibility across independent systems
The purpose of this operation was to observe whether the injection of high-trust metadata into
publicly crawlable surfaces would trigger downstream shifts in how content moderation systems
interpreted entity status. Post-injection, the same content types were re-deployed under
identical posting protocols to test whether report responsiveness, visibility throttling, or account
penalization patterns shifted.
3.3 Report Outcome Tracking
A parallel layer of the methodology involved deliberately triggering the platform’s reporting
response mechanisms. When known botnet-affiliated accounts engaged with posted content,
they were flagged and reported using internal reporting mechanisms (Instagram:
Bullying/Harassment, Spam; TikTok: Misleading Content). Report outcomes were tracked over
7-day windows.
Prior to entity reclassification, over 90% of reports filed by the test account against these
synthetic agents yielded no removal or suspension outcomes. Following schema and metadata
injection, the report outcome ratio shifted sharply—within 48 hours of filing, flagged accounts
began disappearing from platform visibility, often without any notification to the original reporter.
This sudden reversal of moderation outcomes, in the absence of any change in reporting
language, confirmed the presence of entity-based report weighting.
3.4 Longitudinal Visibility Recovery Testing
Post-reclassification, a battery of new content was released using both AI-generated and
human-authored posts, across text, image, and hybrid formats. Initial observations confirmed
persistent suppression inertia—first 3–5 posts after the transition remained sandboxed.
However, on the sixth content cycle, a visibility spike was recorded (+540% impressions
compared to baseline), and the presence of external discovery (non-follower interactions)
reappeared.
This visibility recovery was temporary but reproducible, indicating that once the moderation
classifier begins to detect semantic conflict between high-weight entity status and legacy
suppression records, it temporarily stalls or recalibrates its confidence weighting, allowing partial
signal restoration.
